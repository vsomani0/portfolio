Assignment 4 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Viv Somani — visomani@ucsd.edu

Proposition: The US has an impending crisis in social security caused by increased aging.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Used Percent Changes rather than Difference in Proportions
    Deception score of 1

    • The percent changes exacerbate the differences of the US compared to these other countries since it starts from a lower level. In terms of actual proportions, the change in the US is much more comparable to a country like Japan. However, even an entirely earnest visualizer might prefer using percentages when specifically talking about change.

  • Ignored Overall Population Numbers
    Deception score of 0

    • I did not include the population numbers at all in this case, something a reader may forget about. I could argue that the change in population is more important to discuss the comparative hit of social security instead of the absolute numbers – and it’s also hard to display the raw proportion numbers when we’re discussing change. However, this piece of context would be helpful for a reader to come up with a more informed opinion, so I would consider it a little deceptive.

  • Y-axis doesn’t start at zero
    Deception Score of 2

    • This serves to exaggerate the differences within these countries. This line plot is focused on change, so it’s easy to justify this as an earnest technique. Starting the y-axis at 100 allows us to compare the percent changes better.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Picked France to be right above the US and had an almost parallel growth in recent years
    Deception Score -1

    • This works well and is hard to pick up on. France is a perfectly reasonable country to include in this visualization, but the fact that it’s right above the US and has had a growing senior share minimizes the US’ growth (as opposed to if a stagnant country like Germany was there).

  • Cherry-Picked countries with a larger senior share to be compared to the US
    Deception Score -1

    • This also minimizes the US’ growth since it still seems much below the other countries. However, it's something that a reader can potentially pick up on – so I made sure to not make it egregious and included only highly-populous countries which all have a right to be included.

  • Did not start the y-axis at zero
    Deception score 0

    • I did this not to make it seem like the differences between the US and other countries were very large, but rather to make a reader forget that there are other countries with a lower senior share on first glance. It’s a line plot which often focuses on change – so this is a defensible technique if someone picks up on it.

Final Reflection

It took some iterating to find a convincing visualization on both sides of the proposition without resorting to overly misleading strategies. However, with this, it was interesting to see how much I could share between the two plots (plot style, plot years, plot countries etc.) and still have it be a convincing argument by focusing on a few different metrics. I had already known this partially before when I saw some misleading visualizations refuted by additional context on the internet, but I felt it more viscerally by building it myself.

It’s always hard to define a clear line between an earnest and deceptive visualization technique, especially since visualizations are often meant to prove a point. However, a helpful question for me in evaluating my scores was the following question: if somebody was simply trying to describe the situation, how likely would they be to use (or avoid) this technique? If they’re very unlikely to use a technique, then that’s an indication that the technique is likely deceptive. If an analysis uses several of these deceptive techniques and / or if they have a special incentive to argue for one side, then I would say it's unethical analysis.
